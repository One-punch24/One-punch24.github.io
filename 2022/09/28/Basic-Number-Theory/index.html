1 Well-ordering and factorization

Axioms 1.1 Principles of integer number.

https://proofwiki.org/wiki/Equivalence_of_Well-Ordering_Principle_and_Induction

(1) Well-ordering principle: Every nonempty subset of natural numbers has a least element.

(2) (Weak) Induction principle: Let $S\subset \mathbb{N}$ which satisfies two properties, (a) $0\in S$, (b) $n\in S\Rightarrow n+1\in S$, then $S=\mathbb{N}$.

(3) Strong induction principle.

Proposition 1.2: Well-ordering principle and induction principle are equivalent.

$\mathcal{Proof}.$

$(1)\Rightarrow(2)$: Assume $S\neq \mathbb{N}$ prove that $\mathbb{N} \backslash S=\varnothing$ using well-ordering principle.

$(2)\Rightarrow (3)$ is easy.

$(3)\Rightarrow (1)$ For this part of proof, maybe it is better to recognize the $\mathbb{N}$ and ${P(i)}$ holds for all $i\in \mathbb{N}$ as equivalent. Choose nonempty $Y$ not satisfying well-ordering property. Denote $S={i \notin Y}$, then use (3) show that $S=\mathbb{N}$.

Theorem 2.2 (Euclidean division) Let $n \in \mathbb{Z} $ and $d \in \mathbb{Z}_+$. There exist two integers $q, r \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $n = qd + r$, and $0 \leq r< d$. The integers $q, r$ are unique.

$Proof.$

(1) $\exists 0\leq r<d$, such that $n=qd+r$. Let $S={n-kd}_{k\in \mathbb{Z}}\cap \mathbb{N}$. It is easy to see $S\neq \varnothing$. Thus, there exists a least element $r\in S$ such that $r=n-kd$. Assume $r\geq d$, then $r-1\in S$. Contradiction.

(2) Uniqueness. Suppose $\exists 0\leq r_1q_2$ without loss of generality, $r_1=r_2+(q_2-q_1)d\geq d$. Contradiction.

2.2 Euclidean algorithm

Lemma 2.3 (Euclidean Algorithm) Let $n,q \in \mathbb{Z} $ and $d \in \mathbb{Z}_+$ such that $n = qd + r$, then $gcd(n,d)=gcd(d,r)$.

$Proof.$

(1) If $a|n, a|d$, then $r=a(n/a-d/a)\Rightarrow a|r$.

(2)If $a|d,a|r$, then $n=a(qd/a+r/a)\Rightarrow a|n$.

Since the divisors of $a$ and $−a$ are the same, one can run the Euclidean algorithm for $|a|, |b|$ to find the $gcd(a, b)$.

Corollary 2.5 If $a, b ∈ \mathbb{Z} $ ($a,b \neq 0$) and $d = gcd(a, b)$, then the equation $ax + by = c$, $c \in Z$ has integer solutions for $x, y$ if and only if $c \in d\mathbb Z$.

(1) If there is any integer solution, then it is easy to show $c\in d\mathbb{Z}$.

Corollary 2.6 (Bezout’s Lemma) If $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$ are coprime, then there exist $x, y \in \mathbb{Z}$ such that $ax + by = 1$.

Definition 2.6 (Euler’s phi function) Euler’s totient function $\phi(n)$ is defined for any positive integer $n$ as the number of positive integers $a : 1 \leq a \leq n$ such that $gcd(a, n) = 1$.
